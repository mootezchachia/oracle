## Key Drivers
1. **IRGC cohesion is the master variable** — The regime falls if and only if the security apparatus fractures. Every other factor — economy, protests, bombing — is downstream. The IRGC is ideologically motivated, financially invested in the regime's survival (they control 30-40% of the economy), and has demonstrated willingness to kill civilians en masse (2022). There is zero evidence of fracture.
2. **Ground invasion probability** — The only historical path to regime change within 9 months is full ground invasion + occupation (Iraq 2003). Without it, base rate drops to <10%. The 41.5% Polymarket odds for US forces entering Iran is the key swing variable, but even "entering Iran" ≠ "150,000 troops occupying Tehran."
3. **Time horizon is the No buyer's friend** — 291 days sounds long but regime change is a slow process. The 1979 Iranian Revolution took 14 months from first major protests (January 1978) to Shah's departure (January 1979). The current situation has no comparable internal protest movement yet.

## Risk Factors
- **US full-scale ground invasion** — If the US commits 100,000+ troops to a ground invasion with the explicit goal of regime change, probability jumps to 30-40%. This would be a historically unprecedented political decision requiring Congressional authorization and massive domestic opposition. Risk: moderate (15-20%).
- **IRGC internal fracture** — If mid-level IRGC commanders begin defecting (not just rank-and-file conscripts), the regime could collapse rapidly. This is the "black swan" that could make 38.5% look cheap. But there are zero indicators of this. Risk: low (5-8%).
- **Nuclear escalation forces extreme response** — If Iran attempts to weaponize its nuclear program in desperation and the US responds with bunker-buster strikes on Fordow/Natanz, the resulting chaos could destabilize the regime. Risk: low-moderate (10-12%).
- **Cascading economic + social collapse** — If Kharg destruction leads to hyperinflation, food shortages, and mass protests simultaneously, AND the security forces are overwhelmed by scale, a 2011 Egypt-style collapse is possible. But Egypt's military chose not to fire on protesters — the IRGC has shown no such restraint. Risk: low (5-7%).
- **Trump "deal" that gets marketed as regime change** — Trump could negotiate a face-saving deal (nuclear disarmament + new elections) that Polymarket resolves as "regime fall" depending on resolution criteria. This depends entirely on how the market defines "regime fall." Risk: moderate (10-15%), but resolution criteria likely require actual regime departure.
